## 1. Why Case Studies?

![Finance](/assets/finance.jpg)


In the corporate world, you are rarely given a textbook problem with a clear formula to solve it. Instead, you are given a "situation"—a messy, complex scenario involving people, money, and market forces. 

Employers use case studies during interviews to test three things:
1.  **Analytical Thinking:** Can you identify the root cause of the problem?
2.  **Financial Acumen:** Can you estimate the financial impact?
3.  **Communication:** Can you present your solution clearly?

---

## 2. A Framework for Analysis

![Finance](/assets/finance.jpg)


When presented with a business problem, use the **S-P-I-S** framework to structure your thoughts before you speak:

| Phase | What to Ask Yourself | Output |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **S**ituation | What is the current state of the business? | "Company X is a mid-sized retailer..." |
| **P**roblem | What is the core issue (not just the symptom)? | "...facing a severe cash flow bottleneck." |
| **I**mpact | How much is this costing them (time/money)? | "This is causing delayed vendor payments and a 15% drop in inventory." |
| **S**olution | What are 2 actionable steps to fix it? | "They should renegotiate terms to Net-60 and liquidate slow-moving stock." |

## 3. The Sample Case: "The Growth Trap"

![Finance](/assets/finance.jpg)


**The Scenario:**
*TechGear* is a rapidly growing electronics distributor. For the past two years, their revenue has grown by 40% annually. However, despite record sales, the company is suddenly struggling to pay its employees on time. 
*   **Sales Model:** They sell to large retailers on "Net-90" terms (they get paid 90 days after delivery).
*   **Vendor Model:** They buy inventory from suppliers on "Net-30" terms (they must pay suppliers 30 days after receiving goods).

**The Task:**
You have been hired as a Junior Analyst. The CEO wants to know why a company making millions in sales has no cash in the bank, and how to fix it immediately.
